A couple weeks ago in Lyon, I visited the Resistance and Deportation History Centre – symbolically housed in the former Gestapo headquarters. Lyon was a hub of both repression and resistance, first under the Vichy regime from 1940, and then in 1942 under full Nazi occupation.
I was struck by how central underground communication was to the French resistance. Clandestine printing presses produced newspapers like Combat, Libération, and le Franc-Tireur, each tailored to different audiences – communists, trade unionists, liberal nationalists – fostering a shared sense of purpose, combatting alienation, and coordinating action. The BBC’s Radio Londres, which was illegal to listen to, provided an alternative to Nazi propaganda.
It all reminded me how vital it is to share information during times of injustice. Today, in an overwhelming information ecosystem, the challenge isn’t just knowing who to trust, it’s staying attentive at all. In the midst of so much noise, I hope this modest dispatch can be a steady signal – something clear, if quiet, to tune into.
